Venus

Did you know...

...that Venus and
Earth are similar in size?

Even though Venus is the second closest celestial to the sun, it is the hottest planet in the solar system. The planet is enveloped by a broad atmosphere made up of carbon dioxide, making Venus a huge greenhouse.

The temperature at the surface of the planet is hot enough for lead to melt and for a piece of steel to become red-hot, it is around 470°C. Scientists are trying to understand the greenhouse effect on Earth and the role that carbon dioxide plays by studying Venus.

Mars

Did you know...

...that Mars, like Earth,
is a rocky planet?

Mars is the planet in our solar system where there may have been life or there is microscopic life today. According to research results from several different space probes, you can see evidence that there have been extensive amounts of liquid water.

The scientists are excitedly trying to find out if there was enough ater and time for life to arise before the water - and probably life - disappeared.

Jupiter

Did you know...

...that Jupiter is the largest
planet in the solar system?

The most famous feature on Jupiter - the Great Red Spot - is a huge storm system that was discovered in the 17th century and is still active today. The storm system is twice the size of Earth.

Jupiter has more moons than any other planet in our solar system -79. Galileo saw the four largest - Io, Europe, Ganymede and Callisto using a simple homemade telescope.

Europa

Did you know...

...that Europe is covered in ice?

Europe is the smallest and most lightweight of Jupiter’s moons. One suspects that it has a thick coating of ice over an ocean due to it’s bright and nearly crater-free surface, these suspicions have been reinforced by measurements and computer simulations of Europe’s magnetic field using the Galileo probe.

This makes it one of the most likely locations for extraterrestrial life in our solar system.
